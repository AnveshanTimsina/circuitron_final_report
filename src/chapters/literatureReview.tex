\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}

The automatic recognition of hand-drawn circuit diagrams has been explored since the early 2000s. In one of the earliest studies, Edwards and Chandran \cite{edwards2000machine} proposed an approach that first identified circuit components and then inferred node segments by considering spatial relationships between them. Their system achieved an accuracy of 86\% for component recognition and 92.5\% for node identification, with wire connections traced between identified nodes using hard-coded heuristic conditions. While pioneering for its time, this approach was inherently brittle due to its reliance on manually crafted rules and could not generalize to diverse handwriting styles or circuit topologies. Ramel et al.\ \cite{ramel2000structured} addressed a related problem in the broader domain of technical document recognition, proposing a structured approach that separated graphical primitives from textual annotations followed by structural matching against predefined symbol templates. Belongie et al.\ \cite{belongie2002shape} introduced shape context descriptors for object recognition, computing histograms of relative contour point positions to provide descriptors invariant to small deformations, a useful property for hand-drawn symbols. While these early methods laid the groundwork, they were limited by their inability to learn discriminative features from data, motivating the shift toward deep learning-based approaches.

The detection and classification of individual circuit components is a foundational step in any circuit recognition pipeline, and modern approaches rely heavily on deep learning-based object detection architectures. The R-\gls{cnn} family of detectors introduced region proposals for object detection: Girshick et al.\ \cite{girshick2014rich} proposed R-\gls{cnn} using selective search for candidate regions, followed by Fast R-\gls{cnn} \cite{girshick2015fast} which shared convolutional features across proposals, and Faster R-\gls{cnn} \cite{ren2015faster} which replaced selective search with a Region Proposal Network. He et al.\ \cite{he2017mask} extended this with Mask R-\gls{cnn}, adding instance segmentation capabilities. In the circuit domain, Bayer et al.\ \cite{bayer2023instance} leveraged Mask R-\gls{cnn} for instance segmentation of components in hand-drawn diagrams, achieving approximately 94\% mask accuracy, although they noted that symbol recognition was ``reasonable, yet incomplete,'' particularly in complex drawings with inconsistent handwriting. Single-shot detectors eliminate the region proposal stage for real-time inference; Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2016ssd} introduced SSD, which discretizes the output space into default bounding boxes at multiple scales. The \gls{yolo} family has been especially influential: Redmon et al.\ \cite{redmon2016yolo} proposed the original \gls{yolo} architecture framing detection as a single regression problem, followed by YOLOv2 \cite{redmon2017yolo9000} with batch normalization and anchor boxes, YOLOv3 \cite{redmon2018yolov3} with feature pyramid predictions at three scales, YOLOv4 \cite{bochkovskiy2020yolov4} with Mish activation and Cross-Stage Partial connections, and YOLOv5 \cite{jocher2020yolov5} which gained widespread adoption through its PyTorch implementation. Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2023yolov7} proposed YOLOv7, introducing the (E-\gls{elan}) and model re-parameterization techniques that achieved state-of-the-art results on MS COCO, outperforming all prior real-time detectors in both speed and accuracy. YOLOv7 is the architecture employed in the present project for component detection.

In a 2021 study, Adnan et al.\ \cite{adnan2021cnnbased} developed a two-stage \gls{cnn}-based system for classifying 20 different hand-drawn circuit components, achieving a recognition accuracy of 97.33\%. Reddy and Panicker \cite{reddy2021handdrawn} combined YOLOv5 for component detection with probabilistic Hough transform-based node recognition, achieving a \gls{map} of 98.2\% in component detection and an overall schematic generation accuracy of 80\%, though their system did not handle labeled components. Abhinav et al.\ \cite{abhinav2020electric} proposed a two-step method of detecting and classifying components followed by feeding labeled component strings into an \gls{fsm} for circuit recognition.

Extracting component values such as ``10k$\Omega$'' or ``5V'' from hand-drawn annotations is critical for generating functionally accurate netlists. Shi et al.\ \cite{shi2017crnn} proposed the \gls{crnn} architecture, integrating convolutional feature extraction, bidirectional \gls{lstm} sequence modeling, and \gls{ctc}-based transcription into a unified framework that has become the de facto standard for scene text recognition and forms the basis of the \gls{ocr} module in the present project. Graves et al.\ \cite{graves2006ctc} originally introduced the \gls{ctc} loss function for labeling unsegmented sequence data, with the key advantage that it does not require pre-segmented training data, which is critical for handwritten text where character boundaries are ambiguous. Baek et al.\ \cite{baek2019scene} conducted a comprehensive comparison of scene text recognition models, revealing that attention-based decoders generally outperform \gls{ctc}-based decoders on irregular text while \gls{ctc} remains competitive on regular text, informing the design decision in this project to adopt a \gls{ctc}-based \gls{crnn} for circuit text which tends to be horizontally aligned. More recently, transformer-based architectures such as TrOCR \cite{li2021trocr} have demonstrated strong performance on handwritten text recognition by leveraging pre-trained vision transformers, though they require substantially more computational resources, making them less suitable for lightweight systems like the one proposed here.

Detecting wires and inferring electrical connectivity is arguably the most challenging aspect of circuit diagram recognition, as wire detection must contend with arbitrary orientations, intersections, crossovers, and junctions. The Hough transform, originally proposed by Duda and Hart \cite{duda1972hough}, is a classical technique for detecting straight lines, and its probabilistic variant introduced by Matas et al.\ \cite{matas2000robust} operates more efficiently on random subsets of edge points. Reddy and Panicker \cite{reddy2021handdrawn} used the Probabilistic Hough Transform for wire detection in hand-drawn circuits but encountered difficulties with curved or overlapping wires. Morphological operations such as the parallel thinning algorithm proposed by Zhang and Suen \cite{zhang1984thinning} have also been employed for wire extraction, reducing binary regions to single-pixel-wide skeletons while preserving topological connectivity. Hemker et al.\ \cite{hemker2024schematics} proposed a three-stream approach (one for component detection, one for line and junction detection, and one for text recognition) with outputs combined into a \gls{json} representation from which a netlist is derived. Kelly and Cole \cite{kelly2024digitizing} addressed wire detection in printed circuit schematics, achieving 86.4\% digitization success and 99.6\% \gls{ocr} accuracy, though their system was trained exclusively on computer-generated diagrams and cannot handle hand-drawn variability. An alternative approach formulates wire tracing as a graph search problem where the binarized circuit image is treated as a grid graph and breadth-first search or $A^{*}$ algorithms find paths between component terminals; the present project adopts this path-finding approach after initial experiments with Hough transform-based methods proved inadequate.

Representing circuits as graphs, where nodes correspond to components or connection points and edges represent electrical connections, enables topological reasoning and downstream simulation. Kipf and Welling \cite{kipf2017gcn} introduced Graph Convolutional Networks that generalize convolutions to graph-structured data. Yamakaji et al.\ \cite{yamakaji2023circuit2graph} developed Circuit2Graph, transforming circuit diagrams into graph structures and achieving 97.9\% accuracy on circuit classification tasks using \glspl{gnn}, demonstrating that graph neural networks can effectively model the global structure of a circuit even when visual features alone are insufficient.

The ultimate goal of many circuit recognition systems is to produce a netlist that can drive simulation. Traditional \gls{eda} tools such as KiCad and \gls{ltspice} support netlist export from manually created schematics but do not allow direct image-to-netlist conversion \cite{kicad2024spice}. Sertdemir et al.\ \cite{sertdemir2022img2sim} developed Img2Sim, an artificial neural network-based system that detects components, infers connectivity, and outputs a complete \gls{spice} netlist with over 90\% accuracy in topology extraction, though it requires clean input images and is limited to a predefined set of components. Nagel and Pederson \cite{nagel1973spice} developed \gls{spice} at the University of California, Berkeley, establishing the standard for circuit simulation that persists to this day, with modern derivatives including Ngspice for \gls{dc}, \gls{ac}, and transient analysis, and commercial tools such as PSpice and Simulink. CircuitJS \cite{falstad2026}, a web-based interactive circuit simulator providing real-time visualization of voltage, current, and waveform behavior, is the simulation platform adopted by the present project due to its open format and accessibility.

The emergence of \glspl{llm} has opened new possibilities for circuit understanding and explanation. The CircuitVQA dataset contains more than 115,000 question-image pairs based on 5,725 unique circuits covering approximately 70 symbol types \cite{li2024circuitvqa}, supporting transformer-based \gls{vqa} models that outperform generic systems when fine-tuned on domain-specific data. Vungarala et al.\ \cite{vungarala2024spicepilot} developed SPICEPilot, leveraging GPT-based models to generate and interpret \gls{spice} code, though general-purpose models often fail to respect domain conventions, underscoring the need for domain-adapted models. The present project integrates DeepSeek as an \gls{llm}-based assistant for answering circuit-related queries. Chen et al.\ \cite{chen2011efficacy} studied the efficacy of simulation-based learning in electronics education, finding that interactive circuit simulations significantly improved student comprehension, motivating the design of CIRCUITRON as not merely a recognition tool but an educational platform.

While substantial progress has been made in component recognition, value extraction, connectivity inference, netlist generation, and interactive Q\&A, existing systems remain fragmented or limited in scope. Most systems address only one or two stages of the recognition pipeline; several \cite{kelly2024digitizing, hemker2024schematics} were designed for printed schematics and do not generalize to hand-drawn circuits; none generate an editable, interactive schematic; \gls{vqa} and \gls{llm}-based tools have not been integrated into recognition pipelines; and many systems \cite{reddy2021handdrawn, bayer2023instance} do not extract handwritten component values. CIRCUITRON addresses these gaps by providing a unified, end-to-end pipeline that takes a hand-drawn circuit image as input and produces an editable, simulation-ready digital schematic with integrated \gls{llm}-based assistance.
